# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

### Rules

* No "safe" answers.
* Must be your real belief (not copied from internet).
* Minimum 50 words.

**Hint:** What do you believe about career, money, learning, discipline, relationships, health, success, life, tech industry, etc. that most people don't agree with?

## Answer
One belief I hold that many people around me would disagree with is that you don't have to wait until you're fully prepared before accepting a new opportunity. Many people believe you should first have all the skills, experience, or confidence before saying yes. I believe the opposite: taking on new challenges is often the fastest way to grow because it pushes you to learn, adapt, and improve.

This belief comes from my own experience. Most of the roles I've taken required me to learn new skills from scratch. The beginning was often challenging, but training, hands-on practice, and consistency helped me become confident and deliver results. Looking back, every major step in my career happened because I was willing to start before I felt completely ready.

## Your Article

Imagine reading a feature story about your career three years from now.

Not about who you are today, but about the professional your daily decisions, habits, and consistency are shaping you to become.

One of this week’s assignments in the DevOps Micro Internship (DMI) challenged us to answer a simple question: What does your Version 2.0 look like?

The task was to write as if a journalist were looking back on our career three to seven years from now. It wasn’t about predicting the future; it was about defining the direction we are intentionally building toward.

So, here’s my Version 2.0.

When Ginika Ndubuisi joined the DevOps Micro Internship (DMI) Cohort 3 on June 27, 2026, she had already built a career improving workflows, automating document processes, and supporting businesses behind the scenes. However, technologies such as Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform, Linux, and CI/CD pipelines were completely new to her.

Rather than seeing that knowledge gap as a limitation, she treated it as an opportunity. She built projects, broke them, fixed them, and documented every lesson. Instead of relying solely on tutorials, she learned by applying concepts to real projects and solving problems one step at a time.

Within three years, she had built and shipped production-ready DevOps projects involving cloud infrastructure, Infrastructure as Code, containerized applications, monitoring solutions, and automated CI/CD pipelines. Her GitHub portfolio reflected consistent growth, while the certifications she earned validated the practical skills she had developed through hands-on experience.

She also became known for documenting her journey. Through technical blogs and LinkedIn articles, she shared project walkthroughs, troubleshooting lessons, and practical insights from transitioning into DevOps. Her willingness to share both successes and setbacks encouraged many professionals from non-technical backgrounds to pursue careers in technology.

As a DevOps Engineer, she led automation initiatives that reduced manual work, improved deployment reliability, and streamlined engineering workflows. Beyond her day-to-day responsibilities, she contributed to open-source projects, mentored aspiring engineers, reviewed community projects, and actively supported the DevOps community by sharing practical knowledge.

Looking back, joining DMI Cohort 3 became the turning point in her career. It transformed her from an operations professional into a respected DevOps Engineer, technical writer, mentor, and community contributor. More importantly, her story proved that careers are not defined by where people start but by their willingness to keep learning, building, and showing up consistently.

Whether this story reads like a vision or a future biography, one thing is certain: every skill I learn, every project I build, and every challenge I embrace from today brings me one step closer to becoming this version of myself.
